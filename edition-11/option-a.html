The ASML-TU/e Model
"ASML's decade-long partnership with TU/e, including joint investment in a state-of-the-art cleanroom and support for nearly 100 PhDs, exemplifies how the university can serve as an extended research arm while exploring new governance models for shared infrastructure." — Taylor & Francis, March 3, 2026

ASML doesn't have a research contract with Eindhoven University of Technology. It has a co-owned cleanroom, nearly 100 jointly supervised PhDs, and a co-investment in the physical and human infrastructure of the Brainport regional innovation ecosystem. The relationship is not transactional. It is architectural. The competitive advantage it generates — in talent access, early-stage technology visibility, and exploratory research capacity — is not something any bilateral research contract could produce.

This is the model the most sophisticated R&D organizations are building toward in 2026. Not partnerships. Ecosystems.

Three Capabilities Ecosystems Provide That Contracts Cannot
  • 01Externalized exploratory research at system level. The problems defining competitive advantage in 2026 — AI-physical integration, sustainable materials, energy storage — require simultaneous progress across multiple technical domains. Ecosystem participation makes it possible to engage with that complexity without building the entire research infrastructure internally.
  • 02Genuine talent pipeline access. Organizations that are actively shaping university education — through joint labs, co-supervised doctoral programs, and embedded engineering challenges — are accessing talent before it enters the recruitment market. This is a structural advantage that compounds over time and cannot be replicated through recruitment alone.
  • 03Ecosystem legitimacy and stakeholder alignment. R&D organizations visibly embedded in regional and global innovation ecosystems operate with a degree of regulatory legitimacy and stakeholder access that internally focused organizations do not have. In an environment where public funding and societal license increasingly matter to R&D outcomes, that legitimacy is a competitive asset.
What the Accelerator Market Signals

The global accelerator market reached $5.11 billion in 2025, projected at $6.07 billion in 2026 — driven by AI specialization and an increasingly important structural role: ecosystem anchor. Station F launched F/ai in February 2026 — the first AI accelerator backed simultaneously by OpenAI, Anthropic, Google, Meta, Microsoft, and Mistral, plus AWS, AMD, and Qualcomm. First batch: 20 startups, EUR 34M raised collectively, Deal Day in April 2026.

The signal this sends is not primarily about the individual startups. It is that the world's leading AI organizations have concluded that ecosystem co-investment in early-stage AI development is the right model for technology exploration at the frontier — not purely internal research. The same logic applies to industrial R&D: competitors and complementors can co-invest in shared innovation infrastructure without competing on what that infrastructure produces.

Y Combinator's W26 batch: 14 companies hit $1M ARR by Demo Day — the highest ever for any cohort. 60% of 2026 YC batches are AI companies. These are indicators of where the fastest-moving innovation is happening — outside the large organizations that will eventually adopt it, in ecosystems that were seeded by corporate and institutional co-investment.

Three actions for R&D leadership:

  • 01Map your external partnership portfolio against the ecosystem model. For each significant partnership: is this transactional (we pay for a defined deliverable) or ecological (we co-invest in shared capacity that benefits multiple parties)? That answer tells you how much of your external innovation budget builds competitive infrastructure versus buys services your competitors can equally buy.
  • 02Identify the one ecosystem where your organization should be a co-investor rather than a client. The DOE's Energy Program for Innovation Clusters just awarded $150K to 23 regional incubators to build exactly this kind of ecosystem infrastructure. The question is not whether these ecosystems will form — it is whether your organization is in them.
  • 03Reframe your university partnership governance. If your primary instrument is the sponsored research agreement, you are using a twentieth-century instrument for a twenty-first-century problem. Add joint lab investments, co-supervised doctoral programs, and shared infrastructure contributions to your university engagement portfolio.

DOE EPIC — April 17, 2026

The US Department of Energy's Energy Program for Innovation Clusters (EPIC) awarded $150,000 each to 23 regional incubators and accelerators across the country to build ecosystem infrastructure supporting energy startups and entrepreneurs. Five teams received up to $1M in three-year cooperative agreements. This is government-backed ecosystem architecture — creating the conditions for energy technology innovation to develop regionally rather than in isolated corporate or university silos. For R&D organizations in energy-adjacent sectors, these regional clusters represent both partnership opportunities and early-warning systems for technology developments that will shape their competitive landscape.

Where is your organization on the transactional-to-ecosystem transition?

The organizations that will lead R&D in 2030 are the ones that have built the ecosystem participation model alongside the research contract. I am particularly interested in examples from industrial manufacturing and advanced materials — sectors where ecosystem models are less mature than in pharma and semiconductors but where the opportunity is equally large.
