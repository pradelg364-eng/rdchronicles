Two Gate Reviews
"There is a gate review coming up. The program manager has spent two weeks preparing the slide deck. Everyone around the table knows roughly what the outcome is before the meeting starts — because the preparation process has already filtered out the uncomfortable signals. Sanofi runs its gate reviews differently. Before anyone presents a slide, the AI has already run the what-if scenarios and surfaced the questions the governance team should be asking."

Sanofi's Plai aggregates over one billion data points, participates in every molecule governance meeting, and makes explicit acceleration or halt recommendations. The organizational effect is precise: gate conversations have shifted from validating data inputs to discussing strategy. That is the gate review of 2028. Here is how to prepare for it as an engineer or PM today.

What AI Will Automate

The preparation burden for gate reviews is enormous and largely mechanical: synthesizing market research, compiling competitive landscape data, updating technical feasibility assessments, tracking resource utilization against plan, documenting lessons from similar programs. All of this is pattern-matching across data — work AI can do faster and more comprehensively than any human team.

Stage-Gate Agentic, Dr. Cooper's February 2026 concept, describes AI agents already doing this: drafting gate scorecards from program data, synthesizing voice-of-customer research into structured market evidence, monitoring program health continuously between gates. Early-adopter firms are seeing 50% reductions in development time. The automation is a relief from the most time-consuming and least value-adding part of governance — the presentation preparation that uses up the days before a gate review without adding strategic insight. When AI handles this, human preparation time shifts to where it belongs: thinking about the strategic questions the governance team will ask and how to answer them honestly.

What AI Will Surface That Humans Typically Miss

Human governance systems are episodic by design: you check in at defined gates, assess the program, make a decision. The signals that accumulate between gates — early indicators of technical risk, market shifts, resource pressure — are filtered through the program champion's interpretation and presented in the form that best supports continuation.

AI monitoring systems work continuously and without confirmation bias. They track the signals that the program champion has incentive to downplay. Stage-Gate International documents firms using AI to catch program trouble signals weeks before the gate review would have surfaced them. For engineers and PMs: it is no longer sufficient to have a good story for the gate review. The underlying data has to tell the same story. The AI is watching.

Three changes to how you work when AI is watching:

  • 01Build the data trail from day one. AI governance participants are only as good as the data they can access. Programs documented in structured, accessible formats — requirements in MBSE models, risk registers updated continuously, market assumptions with dates and sources — are the ones where AI adds the most value. Programs documented in slide decks and team memory are invisible to the AI.
  • 02Treat negative evidence as governance gold. In a human governance system, evidence that a program is in trouble gets filtered before reaching the gate. In an AI system, that filtering is harder. Organizations building AI governance participants find that candor is rewarded — the AI will surface the problem anyway, and better to surface it early than have it flagged as an anomaly at the worst possible moment.
  • 03Focus your preparation on judgment-intensive questions. If AI handles data synthesis and pattern-matching, the valuable preparation is: what is the right risk appetite for this program at this stage? What does success look like in three years? What assumptions have we not explicitly tested? These are the conversations AI governance is designed to enable — by handling everything else.

The Candor Effect

One of the most consistent cultural observations from organizations deploying AI governance systems: teams that previously hid problems because the governance culture punished candor are finding that candor is now rewarded. The AI will surface the problem anyway — early, before the gate review, without political filtering. The governance culture that emerges in organizations where this happens is one where surfacing problems early is recognized as good professional practice, not as a threat to program continuation. That cultural shift is worth as much as the efficiency gain.

Is your organization ready for the gate review of 2028?

The gate review of 2028 will be different from the gate review of 2023. The question is not whether AI will be at the table — it is whether you will have the data, documentation, and strategic habits in place to make that governance conversation more productive. Where is your organization in this transition?
