From Decision Support to Governance Participant
"When I first started at Sanofi, R&D discussions focused on validating data inputs. Now, with automation handling the inputs, we can focus more on strategy and forward-looking conversations. AI has driven a significant cultural change and raised the level of dialogue in R&D decision-making." — Sanofi, February 2026

Sanofi built an AI called "Plai." It aggregates more than one billion data points across the company — predicting R&D costs, clinical trial timelines, and the probability of success for every program. It participates in every governance meeting where molecule progression decisions are made, runs "what if" scenarios in real time, and makes explicit recommendations: accelerate this, halt that. This is not a technology story. It is a governance story.

The distinction that separates organizations genuinely transforming their portfolio governance from those with analytics dashboards: decision support is a tool that answers questions you already know to ask. A governance participant surfaces the questions you did not know to ask, before the window to act closes.

Dr. Robert Cooper — the originator of Stage-Gate — is calling this "Stage-Gate Agentic." His February 2026 concept describes AI agents that draft gate scorecards, synthesize voice-of-customer data, monitor program health between gates, and flag anomalies. Early-adopter firms already report 50% reductions in NPD development time. The next frontier: AI that does not just support the gate meeting — it participates in it.

What AI Changes About Portfolio Governance

The Harvard Business School framework maps AI types to governance decisions. Predictive AI — supervised machine learning — excels at pattern recognition: quantifying risk, identifying portfolio gaps, assessing alignment with benchmarks. It is the right tool for portfolio health monitoring, resource allocation modeling, and go/kill scoring. Generative AI synthesizes across knowledge domains to surface connections and evaluate novelty — the right tool for the strategic questions portfolio reviews often miss.

The HBS recommendation: if your goal is sustaining innovation in established technical domains — aerospace, medical devices, industrial equipment — sequence predictive AI first. If your goal is exploratory innovation in shifting markets, the sequencing inverts. Treat AI orchestration as a portfolio decision in itself, with the same rigor as any other strategic technology investment.

MIT Sloan describes 2026 as a "level-set year" — the year AI moves from demonstrating capability to delivering enterprise value. For R&D portfolio governance, the most consequential enterprise value is not accelerating successful programs. It is accelerating the exit from unsuccessful ones — the AI governance participant that systematically watches for early failure indicators and brings them to the governance conversation before sunk cost thinking locks in the decision.

Three actions for R&D leadership:

  • 01Audit your gate scorecard for AI-readiness. Which criteria are currently assessed with data that could be automated? Customer need evidence, competitive landscape, technical feasibility benchmarking, resource availability — these are AI-computable. Protecting the criteria that require human judgment creates space for better strategic conversations about them.
  • 02Define what "AI as governance participant" means for your organization specifically. The Sanofi model works in pharma because the data infrastructure is rich and structured. Your equivalent may differ. Move beyond "AI gives us a dashboard" to "AI has a voice in the meeting that is prepared, consistent, and not subject to political pressure."
  • 03Run one governance cycle with AI-generated pre-read material. Let the AI synthesize program health data, flag anomalies, and generate the questions the governance team should be asking. Evaluate whether the conversation is more strategic or less. That is your leading indicator of whether the AI governance participant model creates value in your context.

The Kill Decision Advantage

The portfolio governance problem most R&D organizations struggle with is not the investment decision — it is the exit decision. AI governance participants that continuously monitor program signals and surface early failure indicators before sunk cost thinking locks in the decision are worth more than any reporting dashboard. Stage-Gate International documents firms catching program trouble signals weeks before the gate review would have surfaced them. The governance value compounds: every quarter of earlier exit on a failing program returns resources to the programs that are winning.

What does "AI as governance participant" look like in your R&D organization?

The organizations that will have the most productive governance conversations in 2028 are building AI governance participants today — not as a technology project but as a strategic capability at the centre of portfolio decision-making.
