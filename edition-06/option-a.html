The Signal You Should Not Miss

"OpenAI and Ginkgo Bioworks tested more than 30,000 experimental conditions over six months. They demonstrated that blending Ginkgo's Reconfigurable Automation Cart cloud lab with the GPT-5 LLM reduces the cost-per-gram of protein production by 40% relative to state-of-the-art methods. Protein yield improved by 27%."

— Nature, March 2026

That is not a pharma story. No human set up a single one of those 30,000 experiments. GPT-5 designed them, ran them, interpreted the results, and decided what to run next — in a closed loop that operated continuously. The result was a 40% cost reduction and a 27% yield improvement. This is the benchmark every R&D-intensive organization should now be measuring itself against.

From Research & Development to Research & Prediction

For three decades, R&D meant running experiments until something worked. The bottleneck was always the same: the physical experiment. Slow, expensive, constrained by human working hours and lab capacity. Agentic AI is dismantling that constraint — not by making experiments faster, but by removing humans from the experiment loop entirely.

Lila Sciences has built what may be the world's largest AI-driven research facility: 22,000 square metres of automated lab space, running R&D as a continuous autonomous service for pharmaceutical and materials science clients. Their system does not just execute experiments — it designs them, interprets results, updates its model, and decides what to run next. Continuously. Without human intervention in the cycle.

The University of Toronto's Acceleration Consortium, backed by a CAD $200 million government grant, operates a fleet of 50 autonomous robots across multiple universities under Alán Aspuru-Guzik. His stated ambition: automate any chemical reaction. "Solve that problem," he says, "and the world is yours."

At Novartis, an internal platform called MicroCycle autonomously synthesizes, purifies, and tests compounds — then analyzes data and selects the next compound to synthesize, without human intervention in the loop. Pharmaceutical Technology called it, in a March 25 feature, one of the platforms approaching Level 5 autonomy: humans only set goals and receive results.

The strategic implication for leadership is precise: the organizations that will lead R&D in 2030 are not the ones with the most brilliant scientists. They are the ones with the best-designed autonomous research pipelines. The human scientist becomes the strategist. The machine becomes the experimenter.

What This Changes About Capital Allocation

The shift from human-led to agent-led experimentation is not a productivity optimization. It is a structural change in how scientific value gets created. When you can run 10,000 experiments for the cost of 100, the rational response is not to run 10,000 experiments on your existing portfolio of questions. It is to ask far more ambitious questions.

The constraint shifts from "what can we afford to test?" to "what is worth testing?" That is a strategic question, not a scientific one — and it belongs in the boardroom, not just the lab.

The talent model changes too. The T-shaped engineer who can orchestrate AI research systems — who has deep enough domain science to judge outputs critically, and enough systems thinking to design the right experimental loops — is worth dramatically more than a specialist who can only run experiments manually. The organizations building this capability now will have a structural advantage within three years.

Three actions for R&D leadership this quarter:

  • 01Map one high-volume experimental workflow — literature synthesis, compound screening, failure mode analysis — and assess whether an agentic layer could run it autonomously. The criterion is not "perfect?" but "good enough to free up my scientists?"
  • 02Audit your data infrastructure — agentic research systems are only as good as the data they can access and the feedback loops they can close. Siloed systems and undigitized lab notebooks are the first constraint to remove.
  • 03Reframe the AI investment question at board level — the question is not "what is our AI budget?" It is "what is our agentic R&D roadmap, and what is the competitive cost of not having one?"

The Shift: From R&D to R&P (Research & Prediction)

The most precise framing of where agentic AI takes R&D comes from the pharma world, where the shift is furthest along. The new model is not "run experiments faster." It is "predict outcomes before you run them — and only build what the prediction says will work." Drug discovery is the leading edge of this. By 2026, the organizations leading life sciences R&D are those that use AI not just as a tool but as an engine to transition discovery from high-risk trial-and-error to calculated, predictable forecast. Every R&D-intensive sector — materials, manufacturing, chemicals, energy — is 12 to 36 months behind pharma on this curve.

Is your R&D organization structured to capture machine-speed insights — or still running on human-pace governance?

The lab that never sleeps is operational. The strategic gap is not technology — it is organizational readiness. I am particularly curious about examples from industrial R&D and advanced manufacturing, where the self-driving lab model is less discussed but the opportunity is just as large.
